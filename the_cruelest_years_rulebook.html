Theater of Operations

Overview of the conflict, the period, and the burden of command.

The world stands at the edge of its second great catastrophe. Empires falter, ideologies sharpen, and the appetite for land, gold, and glory has not yet been answered. In The Cruelest Years, each player assumes the role of a head of state between October 1935 and September 1945 — the decade that broke the modern world. Every turn you must balance the forge, the field, the kitchen table, and the treasury. To lead a nation through these years is to choose, again and again, between what is necessary and what is decent.

This is not a game of conquest alone. A commander whose armies sweep the continent but whose people riot in the streets will fall as surely as one outflanked in the field. Victory rewards the leader who learns to govern, not merely to fight.

The Central Tension

Imperialism, colonialism, and nationalism have inflamed a generation of grievances over land, capital, and prestige. Every nation in the game holds at least one of these grievances; many hold all three. The game asks: will your statecraft serve the dignity of your people, or be devoured by the appetite that started the war?

Designer's Intent — The Cruelest Years is meant to be replayable across many factors so that no two games unfold alike. The mechanisms reward foresight: nearly every action has a second-order consequence in another column of the ledger. A leader who builds factories without feeding cities will be deposed; a leader who feeds cities without arming the frontier will be invaded. The hard part is doing both.

The Calendar of War

In which one turn is one month, and ten years lie between the first shot and the last.

Each turn represents one calendar month. The game begins at the historical opening of the global conflict and ends at the Pacific surrender. Players may agree to play a shorter campaign by selecting any starting and ending month within the range.

Order of a Turn

  1. Event Phase. Resolve any historical event triggered by the current month (see § IX).
  2. Production Phase. Each factory produces its goods. Treasury and population grow or decline according to historical trend cards.
  3. Action Phase. Each player, in turn order, takes actions: build units, move forces, declare war, sign pacts, raise taxes.
  4. Upkeep Phase. Pay daily necessities to feed the population. Adjust happiness. Check for revolution and inflation thresholds.
  5. Advance the Calendar. Move to the next month.
House Rule (Short Campaign) — For a shorter game, begin in September 1939 (Invasion of Poland) and end at the player's choice. For a single sitting, end at May 1945 (V-E Day) and treat the Pacific theater as resolved by treaty.

— SECTION IV —

Industrial Capacity

Factories are the engine of every campaign. Without them, neither armies nor people endure.

Every nation operates two classes of factory. They are the heartbeat of its economy and the most consequential tile a player will ever place on the board. Factories cost treasury to build, take turns to complete, and are vulnerable to bombing.

⚙
Military Factory
Produces 1 weapon per turn. Weapons are the raw material of warfare — they combine with population units to form every military unit listed in § VII. A nation without military factories cannot raise an army worth the name.
⌂
Industrial Factory
Produces 1 daily necessity per turn. Daily necessities feed and clothe the population — without them, happiness collapses and revolution follows. The factory of bread is no less decisive than the factory of bullets.

Building Units from Weapons

A weapon and a population unit combine to form a soldier, tank, plane, or boat. Different unit types require different combinations of weapons, population, and time — refer to the Order of Battle in § VII for the full cost sheet. The act of combining ends the turn for those resources; they cannot be moved or used elsewhere in the same month.

Choke Point — A nation that produces only weapons will starve. A nation that produces only daily necessities will be conquered. Balance is enforced not by the rules, but by the consequences.

— SECTION V —

The Home Front

Population, daily necessities, and the limits of what a people will endure.

Population

1 population unit = 1 million people. Every nation begins with a historical population (see § III) and grows or declines each turn according to its trend card. Population is spent to build units, lost to combat and famine, and is the ultimate measure of a nation's capacity to wage war.

Daily Necessities

Each turn, every nation must pay 1 unit of daily necessities for every million people in its population. Necessities are produced by industrial factories or imported through trade. Shortfalls are deducted from happiness.

Population Happiness

Happiness measures whether your people support the regime. It begins at 100% and falls as necessities are missed, casualties mount, or harsh decrees are passed. As happiness drops, the home front begins to crack.

75%
Steady. The population accepts the war effort. No penalties to production or morale.
50%
Strained. Murmurs of dissent. Civil unrest is visible but contained. Mark the threshold and watch closely.
25%
Cracking. Production grinds. All unit construction takes one additional turn.
0%
Revolution. The regime falls. The nation passes to civil-war status and is removed from the active war effort for a determined number of turns.
The Quiet Front — A leader who wins every battle but loses their capital to bread riots has not won. Revolution is the most expensive defeat in this game.

— SECTION VI —

Treasury & Inflation

In which gold is printed, debts come due, and the ledger keeps its own kind of war.

Each nation maintains a treasury, denominated in units of gold and credit. Treasury grows or declines each turn according to the nation's historical trend. It funds factories, units, diplomacy, and the cost of every choice the commander makes.

Printing More

A nation may choose to inject more treasury mid-game — a stand-in for war bonds, debased currency, and forced loans. This is permitted, but it always raises inflation. Inflation reduces the purchasing power of all future treasury, making every subsequent build more expensive.

The Zero Line — If a nation's treasury reaches zero, inflation is automatically pushed back to a designated reset value. This is not relief — it is a controlled collapse, and the cost is borne by the people in lost purchasing power for many turns to come.

Why It Matters

A wartime economy that prints too freely will hollow itself from within. A wartime economy that prints too little will run out of weapons. The treasury is the lever that connects every other system in the game: factories cannot be built without it, units cannot be raised without it, and even diplomacy is paid in coin.

— SECTION VIII —

Rules of Engagement

Combat, stacking, transport, and the targeting of the skies.

Movement & Speed

A unit's speed determines how many tiles or zones it may traverse in a single action phase. Combining weapons and population to construct a unit ends the action for those resources that turn — the new unit cannot move until the following month.

Range & Attack

Range is the maximum distance at which a unit may strike. Attack is the damage it deals upon a successful engagement. Blood is the unit's resilience — when it is reduced to zero, the unit is removed from the board.

Naval Transport

Boats serve as the only means of transporting land forces across water. Every boat carries a number of troop units equal to its treasury fee — a light boat carries 2 in the Field Roster, a heavy boat 3, an aircraft carrier 6. A sunk boat carries its passengers down with it.

Air Warfare

Aircraft operate in their own envelope. Only planes and Anti-Aircraft Artillery may target aircraft. All other units must endure aerial bombardment without reply. Heavy bombers in particular can shatter industrial centers — a single strike on a factory complex may set production back several turns.

Aircraft Carriers — have no inherent range or attack of their own. Their purpose is to project planes far from the home coast. Planes launched from a carrier use the carrier's tile as their point of origin.

Stacking Rules

In both rosters, identical units may be stacked together on a single stand. In the Standard Roster, statistics scale according to the listed 1 / 2 / 3 values. In the Field Roster, only blood and attack stack; range, speed, and other attributes are taken from the unit's base entry regardless of stack depth.

Chronicle of War

In which the calendar itself becomes a player at the table.

The world does not pause for the convenience of the commander. As certain months are reached, historical events are revealed and resolved during the Event Phase of the turn. They may trigger new wars, force diplomatic decisions, change resource trends, or alter the morale of populations. Some events fire automatically; others depend on the state of the board.

Three Hinges of History

Among the dozens of events in the deck, three are weighted as decision points — moments at which a single different choice could have changed the entire course of the war. Each is presented to the relevant players with a choice, and the cost of the choice ripples through the rest of the game.

I
The German – Soviet Non-Aggression Pact
II
Germany Invades the Soviet Union
III
Japan Attacks Pearl Harbor
Replayability — Because the events deck branches on player choices and on the state of the board, no two games unfold in the same way. The same hinge may produce alliance, betrayal, or stalemate depending on what the table has already done.

Designer's Field Notes

A candid record of the questions every wargame about this war must answer.

No game set in the years 1935–1945 can be made innocently. The designer of The Cruelest Years wrote down the questions below before placing a single counter on the board. They are reproduced here so that every commander who plays the game knows what was being attempted, and what was being refused.

What historical event are you considering?
The Second World War, from the Italian invasion of Ethiopia in October 1935 to the Japanese surrender in September 1945.
What is the central conflict or tension?
Growing imperialism, colonialism, and nationalism produced overlapping conflicts of land, money, and prestige between countries throughout the world. The war was the spillover of those appetites.
Who are the major players — winners, losers, and those affected but not directly involved?
Winners — the Allies: the United States, the Soviet Union, France, England, China, and others.
Losers — the Axis: Germany, Italy, Japan, and their co-belligerents.
Others affected: the smaller European and Asian states caught between the great powers, the colonies, and the civilian populations of every nation.
What was at stake? What did people stand to win or lose?
At the level of leaders, the stake was land, money, and power. At the level of populations, the stake was survival — of cities, of families, of national identity, and in many cases of life itself.
Is there one moment where a different decision could have changed everything?
There are several. The game treats three as formal decision points: the German – Soviet Non-Aggression Pact, the German invasion of the Soviet Union, and the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor. Each is a hinge at which the rest of the war turned.
Why does the event work as a game?
Because it teaches players to feel the second-order consequences of their choices. With many interacting systems — military, industrial, demographic, monetary, diplomatic — every game produces a different war. The lesson is not which side won; the lesson is how the choices accumulate.
What could go wrong?
The hardest design problem is how to represent the genuine brutality of the Axis regimes — atrocities, occupation, genocide — without sanitizing them and without turning them into entertainment. The game does not allow these acts to be "performed" as moves; it represents their costs as fixed historical penalties and through the events deck.
Is it ethical for this to be a board game?
The event is ethical to use as a game so long as the approach is comprehensive and educational — to learn from history's mistakes, not to re-enact them as spectacle. The rulebook, the events deck, and the designer's notes are written with that intention.
