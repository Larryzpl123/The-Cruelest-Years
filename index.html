— SECTION I —

Theater of Operations

Overview of the conflict, the duties of command, and how a war is won.

The world stands at the edge of its second great catastrophe. Empires falter, ideologies sharpen, and the appetite for land, gold, and glory has not yet been answered. In The Cruelest Years, each player takes one of eighteen nations to the field of the Second World War. You command its leaders, raise its armies, hold its industry, and steer its fate across a board of forty-one territories. Victory is measured in the soil you hold when the guns fall silent.

This is the streamlined ruleset: fast to teach, fast to play, asymmetric by design. Every nation has its own leader, its own national trait, and its own strategic shape — all detailed in the Stats & Cards document.

Multi-Theatre Play — When a game spans more than one theatre, the thresholds for each theatre played are added together. A game covering Europe + Asia requires 18 territories for solo victory (12 + 6) or 23 for team victory (15 + 8). This lets the table assemble a campaign of exactly the scale they want to play.
The Central Tension — Imperialism, colonialism, and nationalism have inflamed a generation of grievances over land, capital, and prestige. Every nation in the game holds at least one of these grievances; many hold all three. The game asks not who is right, but who can hold the ground when the smoke clears.

The Calendar of War

One turn is one month; ten years lie between the first shot and the last.

Each turn represents one calendar month. The game opens at the historical spark of the global conflict and may run as long as the table desires; the full historical span is October 1935 to September 1945.

★ The Spark ★
OCT 1935
Italy invades Ethiopia.
Turn 1 begins.
⟶ ★ ⟶
★ The Silence ★
SEP 1945
Japan surrenders.
Full campaign end.

Turn Order — The First Move

Because Italy struck the first blow of the war (October 1935, Ethiopia), Italy takes the first turn of the game. The Italian player then flips a coin to determine the direction of play:

  • Heads — play proceeds clockwise around the table.
  • Tails — play proceeds counter-clockwise.
Reversal Rule — At the start of every new round, the direction of play reverses. The last player of one round becomes the first player of the next, and so on. This prevents the same player from always reacting to the same neighbour, and prevents end-of-round kingmaking.

Order of a Turn — Four Phases

Each player resolves the following phases, in order, on their own turn:

  1. Income and Products Phase. Collect gold, population, and weapons from every city and factory under your control. Cities produce +5 gold and +1 population per turn. Factories produce +1 weapon per turn. (Per-nation starting counts are in the Stats document.)
  2. Leader Phase. Use any leader or territorial ability. Passive abilities are always working and need not be declared; active abilities must be triggered now and may have a cost (gold, weapons, population) or a cooldown (CD) preventing reuse for a stated number of turns. (All ability text is in the Stats document.)
  3. Training Phase. Decide which military units to recruit and in which controlled territories to place them. A unit costs the weapons and population shown on the unit table. Newly trained units cannot move or attack on the turn they are created; they enter service next turn.
  4. Action Phase. Move and attack with previously trained units. A unit may attack after moving, but may not move after attacking unless its ability says otherwise.
House Rule (Short Campaign) — For a single sitting, begin in September 1939 (Invasion of Poland) and end whenever the table agrees, or by the first solo-victory threshold reached. For a quick demo game, play any scenario for 12 turns and award the win to whoever holds the most territory at the end of the year.

Special Rule · The Two Chinas

China occupies a unique political position in this period — a fragile coalition between the Nationalist government (KMT) under Chiang Kai-shek and the Communist resistance under Mao Zedong, both fighting Japan and each other. China may be played in any of three modes, agreed upon before the campaign begins.

壹
Unified China
A single player commands all of China's resources, units, and decisions. Both leader cards (Mao and Chiang) are available to that one player.
貳
United Front
Two players share China. Both have full decision rights; treasury and unit pool are shared. Each plays one of the two Chinese leaders. Disagreements on major actions are resolved by die roll.
參
Civil Truce
The dossier splits 60 / 40 between Nationalist (KMT, Chiang) and Communist (CCP, Mao). They may cooperate, betray, or remain in armed truce while jointly facing Japan.

Several nations — Korea, British India, and the Pacific dominions — begin under the control of another power and have special rules to play independently. These are detailed in § IX, Occupied Nations.

— SECTION IV —

Setup

Follow these steps in order. A new player should be able to reach Turn 1 by following them once.
  1. Place the World Map in the centre of the table so all players can reach their starting territories.
  2. Choose nations. Each player chooses one of the eighteen playable powers. With fewer than six players, give priority to the Great Powers (Germany, UK, France, Italy, Soviet Union, USA, China, Japan) before regional powers. Unassigned nations are controlled by the rules or left neutral.
  3. Distribute starting resources. Each player takes the gold, weapons, and population listed in their nation's dossier (Stats document).
  4. Place starting cities and factories. Place each nation's city and factory markers on the specific named territories listed in the Stats document's placement tables.
  5. Place starting units. Each player places their starting military units (default Infantry) on their home territories. Players may exchange any Infantry for another unit of equal or lower weapon cost at no extra cost.
  6. Choose a leader. Each nation has between one and three named historical leaders (Stats document). Each player chooses one leader to govern with for the entire campaign. The choice is binding except through specific event cards.
  7. Determine first turn. Italy takes the first turn. The Italian player flips a coin: heads for clockwise, tails for counter-clockwise play.
  8. Begin Turn 1. The Italian player begins the Income and Products Phase.
Note on Starting Asymmetry — Players do not start with equal resources. This is a conscious design choice. In autumn 1935, the world's industrial, military, and economic strength was distributed extremely unevenly — the United States held over a hundred billion dollars in real assets while the Republic of China struggled to maintain a national army. The game preserves this asymmetry to teach what was at stake. Balance is provided not by equal starts but by the team-victory mechanic, the multi-region campaign system, and asymmetric leader cards.

— SECTION V —

Cities, Factories & Resources

The three resources of war — gold, population, weapons — and the structures that produce them.

The economy of The Cruelest Years rests on three resources and two kinds of structure. The math is simple by design — the strategy lies in holding the cities and factories you have, and in capturing the enemy's.

The Three Resources

🟡
Gold (Treasury)
The currency of the nation. Pays for ability costs, unit maintenance, and any agenda card with a money cost.
👥
Population
Manpower. Each new military unit consumes one or more population at training. Population is also the long-term measure of national strength.
🔫
Weapons
The output of factories. Combined with population, weapons form military units according to the unit table in the Stats document.

Cities — The Civil Engine

Each city under your control produces +5 gold and +1 population per turn. Cities are placed on specific named territories at game start (see the placement tables) and cannot be moved or rebuilt. If a city is captured by an enemy, its income belongs to the captor on the following turn. One city in each nation is designated the Capital; most event and plan cards reference it.

Factories — Fixed for the War

Factories produce 1 weapon per turn, every turn, with no choices to make. Each nation begins with the factories printed in its dossier, placed on specific named territories (see the placement tables).

Factories Cannot Be Built. In this edition, no new factories may be constructed during play. The world's industrial map was set before the war began. Factories exist only where they start — and the only way to gain a factory is to capture the territory it sits on. When you occupy an enemy territory containing a factory, that factory is yours and begins producing weapons for you on your next Income and Products Phase. When you lose the territory, you lose the factory.
Industry is Geography — Because factories are fixed, the great industrial centres — Berlin, the Ruhr, the Donbas, Detroit, Shanghai, the English Midlands — are the most valuable territory on the board. A nation that captures Essen or Stalingrad does not merely take ground; it takes the enemy's ability to arm itself. Defend your factory cities above all else.

Maintenance Fees

Every military unit on the board costs gold each turn to keep in the field, paid during the Income and Products Phase. Per-unit fees are listed in the unit table. A unit whose maintenance cannot be paid is removed from the board; the owner chooses which units to remove if several would be unsupported.

— SECTION VI —

Training & Military Units

How to raise the nine military units. Full statistics in the Stats document.

There are nine military units in the game: Infantry, Mechanized Infantry, Artillery, Tank, Boat, Aircraft Carrier, Plane, Bomber, and Anti-Aircraft Artillery. Each has its own cost and battlefield profile, listed in the unit table of the Stats document. National Unique Unit Traits modify these base values for each nation.

Training a Unit

Units are recruited during the Training Phase of your turn:

  1. Pay the unit's Weapon and Population cost from your stockpile (see the unit table). Partial payment is not allowed.
  2. Place the figure, marked untrained, on any territory you control.
  3. The unit completes training at the start of your next turn. It cannot move or attack on the turn it is recruited.
No Stacking — Each tile (territory) may contain only one military unit at a time. There is no piling-up of tokens. Concentrate striking power by sequencing attacks, not by stacking units.

Transport & Aircraft

Two unit families have special rules that the rest of the rulebook references:

  • Boats and Aircraft Carriers carry land units across water. A Boat carries land units equal to its Fee; an Aircraft Carrier carries up to six land units and also launches friendly Planes from open ocean.
  • Aircraft (Planes, Bombers) can only be targeted by other Planes and by Anti-Aircraft Artillery. All other units must endure aerial attack without reply. Bombers can destroy the factory in a territory they strike.
Where the Numbers Live — This rulebook deliberately contains no unit statistics. The full table of Weapon / Population / HP / Range / Attack / Speed / Fee for all nine units, plus every national modifier, is in the Stats & Cards document so it can be revised without reprinting the rules.

— SECTION VII —

Rules of Engagement

Movement, combat, range, and the targeting of the skies.

Movement & Speed

A unit's Speed (from the unit table) is the number of tiles it may traverse in a single Action Phase. A unit may attack after moving, but may not move after attacking, unless a leader ability or trait specifically permits.

Combat & HP Coins

Each unit's HP is tracked by HP coins placed beneath the figure. When a unit is attacked, remove HP coins equal to the attacker's Attack value. A unit with no remaining HP coins is destroyed and removed from the board. When two units attack each other simultaneously, both deal their damage in the same combat resolution; if both reach zero HP, both are removed.

Range & The Over-Range Strike

Range is the maximum number of tiles between attacker and target for a normal strike. A commander may also attempt an over-range strike: declaring an attack against a target one tile beyond the unit's printed Range. To succeed, flip a coin.

  • Heads — the over-range strike hits normally.
  • Tails — the strike misses; the attack is wasted but the unit's action is still spent.

Over-range strikes may only extend a unit's Range by one tile. They cannot chain.

Capturing Territory & Factories

When you move a unit into an enemy or neutral territory and no enemy unit remains to defend it, the territory becomes yours. Any city or factory on that territory transfers to you and begins producing for you on your next Income and Products Phase (see § V). This is the only way to gain a factory.

Air Warfare

Aircraft operate in a layer of their own. Only Planes and Anti-Aircraft Artillery may target aircraft. Bombers can destroy the factory in a territory they strike, removing the enemy's weapon production at its source.

Aircraft Carriers — The carrier's self-defence statistics are minimal (see the unit table). Its real value is two-fold: it launches friendly Planes from deep ocean, and it carries up to six land units across water. Without carriers, a Pacific war is impossible to win.

— SECTION VIII —

Leaders, Abilities & National Traits

How abilities work. The full text of every leader and trait is in the Stats document.

Every nation carries three signatures of its own: a chosen leader, a national (territorial) ability, and a Unique Unit Trait. Together they make the game asymmetric. This section explains the mechanics of how abilities work; the content of all eighteen nations' cards is in the Stats & Cards document.

Choosing a Leader

Each nation has one to three named historical leaders. At setup, each player chooses one leader to govern with for the entire campaign. The choice is binding — there is no swap mid-game except via a specific event card.

The Three Ability Types

✶
Passive
Always active. Triggers automatically whenever its condition is met. Does not need to be declared and has no cooldown.
⚡
Active
Must be declared during your Leader Phase. May have a cost (gold, weapons, population) and a cooldown (CD) — the number of turns before it can be used again.
⚔
Hybrid
Combines a permanent passive effect with an activatable component declared during the Leader Phase.

Reading Cooldowns

A cooldown of 1 means the ability can be used every other turn. A cooldown of 5 means once every five turns. A very high cooldown (e.g. 99) effectively limits the ability to once per game. Track cooldowns with the cooldown dials provided.

Every Card's Text Lives in the Stats Document — To keep this rulebook stable while the cards are still being balanced, all leader abilities, national traits, and unit modifiers are printed in the Stats & Cards document. Open it alongside this one.

— SECTION IX —

Occupied Nations & Dominions

Korea, British India, and the Pacific dominions begin under another power. Here is how — and when — they may act for themselves.

Several playable nations did not control their own destiny in the 1930s. Korea was a Japanese colony; India and the Pacific dominions answered to the British Crown. The Cruelest Years models this directly: these nations are playable, but they begin subordinate to a "master" power and can only act freely once they have won their independence in play.

Korea — Under Japanese Occupation

Korea begins fully occupied by Japan. A player who chooses Korea does not act as a free nation at the start. To play Korea independently, the Korean player (or their allies) must first defeat Japan in revolt — that is, drive Japanese units out of the Korean home territory and hold it. Until Korea is liberated:
  • Korean territory, cities, and the single Korean factory produce for Japan, not for the Korean player.
  • The Korean player may not train or move units freely; they may only build resistance using the Korean leader's Resistance Network ability (see Stats document).
  • Once Korean home territory is cleared of Japanese units and held for one full round, Korea becomes a free nation and plays normally for the rest of the game.

British India & the Dominions — Under the Crown

British India, and the Pacific dominions (Australia and New Zealand within Oceania), begin subordinate to the United Kingdom. To play British India — or to play Australia / Oceania independently of Britain — that player must first revolt against British control: declare independence and survive one full round without losing their capital to British forces. Until independence is won:
  • The subordinate nation must follow the orders of the British (UK) player on major actions — which wars to join, which territories to defend — exactly as a dominion historically deferred to London.
  • The subordinate nation keeps its own income and units, but cannot declare war on, or refuse to defend against, a target chosen by the UK player.
  • Once independence is declared and held for one full round, the nation acts entirely for itself and may even turn against its former master.
Why This Rule Exists — The histories of Korea, India, and the dominions in the Second World War were shaped by exactly this tension: vast populations and real military contributions, made under the political control of a foreign or imperial master, with independence as the prize that the war itself put within reach. The revolt rule is the game's way of giving these nations agency without erasing the fact that they began the period without it.
Cooperative & Advanced Play — Because they begin subordinate or occupied, Korea, British India, and an independent Oceania are best suited to experienced players, or to cooperative games where a Great Power ally helps engineer their liberation. A new player is usually better served by one of the eight Great Powers.

Agendas, Policies & Historical Plans

An optional deck of strategic doctrines layered on top of leader cards. Still in design.

Beyond their leader cards and unit traits, each nation will hold three Agenda cards representing the deliberate strategic doctrines of its wartime leadership (Germany's Blitzkrieg, the Soviet Scorched Earth, America's Lend-Lease, and so on). These are optional — the streamlined game is complete without them — and they add a second layer of strategy.

How a Card Works

  1. Activation. Declare during the Leader Phase. Pay any cost in gold, weapons, population, or units.
  2. Effect. Resolve immediately, or persist for the stated number of turns.
  3. Cooldown. Once played, set the card aside for its stated cooldown.
  4. Conditional Plans. Some cards may only be played if a precondition is met (e.g. "must be at war with a Great Power").
The Card Library is in the Stats Document — The full deck of agenda cards (three per nation) is being designed and balanced. The current draft, with the Blitzkrieg worked example and all provisional cards, is printed in the Stats & Cards document.

Chronicle of War

An optional deck of historical events that fires on calendar dates.

An optional Historical Events Deck reflects the real calendar of the war — events that altered alliances, opened fronts, or forced decisions. Some events fire automatically on their historical date; others must be played by a commander whose prerequisite conditions have been met.

Three Hinges of History

Three events are weighted as decision points — moments where a different choice could have changed the rest of the war.

II
Germany Invades the Soviet Union

Events Deck — In Development. The full deck (target ~30 events spanning 1935–1945) is still being designed. Each event will list a trigger date, a precondition (if any), and a resolution effect. The streamlined game plays cleanly without the deck; events are an optional layer for groups that want a longer, more historically textured campaign.

Campaign Scenarios

The full world war, or a smaller theatre. The same rulebook, scaled to the time you have.

The Cruelest Years is built around the global campaign, but few groups have ten hours to spare. Each scenario below is a self-contained module — a fixed roster of belligerents, a fixed start month, and a recommended player count. The same rules apply; only the map and the victory threshold change.

Turns: Oct 1935 → Sep 1945 (120 turns).
Players: 4 – 8 (regional powers as wildcards or extra seats).

Premise: Tutorial-length introduction. Use for first plays.
Designer's Note — Theatre modules are not miniature versions of the full game; they are real campaigns at smaller scale. Each ships with its own subset of the events deck (Pearl Harbor is pre-resolved in the Asian module, etc.) and its own balance adjustments. The full 120-turn flagship is the only way to feel the strategic linkage between fronts — Stalingrad relieving Britain, Pearl Harbor dragging America into Europe.

— SECTION XIII —

Designer's Field Notes

A candid record of the questions every wargame about this war must answer.

No game set in the years 1935–1945 can be made innocently. The designers wrote down the questions below before placing a single counter on the board, and revisit them with every edition. They are reproduced here so that every commander who plays the game knows what was being attempted, and what was being refused.

What historical event are you considering?
The Second World War, from the Italian invasion of Ethiopia in October 1935 to the Japanese surrender in September 1945. 120 turns of one month each.
What is the central conflict or tension?
Growing imperialism, colonialism, and nationalism produced overlapping conflicts of land, money, and prestige between countries throughout the world. In the streamlined edition the central tension is operationalised as a contest for territory — and especially for the fixed industrial centres that cannot be rebuilt once lost.
Who are the major players?
Eighteen nations, weighted to the European and Asian theatres where the war was fought hardest. Nine European powers; two Asian; seven distributed across the Americas, Africa, the Pacific, the Middle East, and South Asia. Each nation carries one or more named historical leaders.
What was at stake?
At the level of leaders, the stake was land, money, and power. At the level of populations, the stake was survival. The streamlined game expresses this as the simple, brutal question: who holds the ground when the smoke clears?
Is there one moment where a different decision could have changed everything?
There are several. The optional events deck (§ XI) treats three as formal decision points: the German – Soviet Non-Aggression Pact, the German invasion of the Soviet Union, and the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor.
Why does the game work?
Because the asymmetry is real. Eighteen leader pools, eighteen unit traits, fixed industrial geography, and a coin-flip turn-direction at game start mean every campaign tells a different story. The streamlined rules keep the moving parts few; the variety lives in the cards, not the bookkeeping.
What could go wrong?
Two persistent design problems. First, the brutality of the Axis regimes — atrocities, occupation, genocide — cannot be performed as moves and is not present mechanically. The streamlined game represents the cost only through territory loss, the occupation rules (§ IX), and historical events. Second, balance: some leader cards are visibly stronger than others and will require playtest revision. The current solution is the team-victory mechanic, which lets weaker neighbours combine against a strong central power.
Is it ethical for this to be a board game?
The event is ethical to use as a game so long as the approach is comprehensive and educational — to learn from history's mistakes, not to re-enact them as spectacle. The rulebook, the events deck, and these designer's notes are written with that intention.
