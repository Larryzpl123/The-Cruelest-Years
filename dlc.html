What's in This Document

The base game emphasizes Europe. Everything else lives here.

The base game of The Cruelest Years is European. It plays in about an hour, with rules a new player can absorb in 10 minutes. The Second World War, however, was not only European. The Asian theatre, the occupied nations, and several advanced combat options each add real complexity. We have moved them here so the base game stays approachable, and so the table can choose how deep it wants to go.

Each section below is a self-contained expansion. Use any combination at the start of a game by agreement of all players. Once chosen, the rules below override the base game.

Over-Range Strike

Push your guns just a little farther than they were built for.

In the base game, your unit's Range is final. You attack within Range, or you do not attack at all. The Over-Range Strike rule lets you push beyond — at the cost of a coin flip.

The Rule

During the Move & Attack phase, a unit may attempt to attack a target one unit of distance beyond its printed Range. Declare the over-range attack, then flip a coin.

★
Heads
The over-range strike hits normally. Apply your Attack value as damage. The attack costs you the unit's action for the turn (it cannot move after attacking).
✗
Tails
The strike misses. No damage is dealt. The unit's action is still spent for the turn — over-range attempts cost the attempt, not just the hit.
Limit — Over-range strikes may extend a unit's Range by one tile only. They cannot chain or combine with any other range-extending ability.

Why This Lives in the DLC

The over-range strike adds a layer of risk-management to every attack. It rewards experienced players who understand probability and over-extension. It also slows down combat, because every borderline attack needs a coin flip. The base game omits it so first-time players can resolve combat without arithmetic on top of randomness.

Inflation — Print the Money Now, Pay Later

Trade tomorrow's economy for today's army.

Every government in the Second World War borrowed from its future to fight its present. Germany printed Reichsmarks. Britain liquidated its empire. The United States ran the largest deficit in its history. The Inflation rule gives every nation that same option — and the same long-term cost.

The Ability

Print the Money. During your Use Abilities phase, you may activate Inflation by adding +10 gold to your treasury immediately. You may activate Inflation once per turn (no cooldown).

The Cost

Each activation of Inflation places one Inflation Marker on your nation card. Inflation Markers do not go away. From the turn after you activate Inflation, and forever after, you take the following penalty during your Collect phase:

📉
Per Marker · Per Turn
Lose 1 gold from your treasury for every Inflation Marker you hold. If you have no gold to lose, lose 1 weapon instead. If you have no weapons, lose 1 population.
∞
No Cure
Inflation Markers cannot be removed for the rest of the game. The only way out is winning before the markers eat you.

The Math

One Inflation Marker pays for itself after 10 turns of loss. Two markers (you've printed +20 gold total) pay for themselves after 10 turns each, costing 2 gold per turn forever. The strategy is short-term — it works if you can win the game in fewer than 10 turns of the activation. Past that horizon, you are paying a permanent tax.

When to Use It

  • Surprise offensive. You're 5 turns from a likely victory and need one more wave of units. Print and strike.
  • Last-ditch defense. The Wehrmacht is at your border. You need a Tank now, not next turn. Print and conscript.
  • Emergency rescue. An ally is collapsing. You can fund a relief army at the cost of your post-war economy.
Why this isn't free. Inflation rewards aggression and short campaigns. In a long, drawn-out game, the player who printed three or four Inflation Markers will starve out their own army by turn 20. The temptation to print "just one more" has destroyed real economies. Don't let it destroy yours.

The Two Chinas

A Nationalist government and a Communist resistance, fighting Japan — and each other.

In the 1930s, the Republic of China was not a single state. The Nationalist government under Chiang Kai-shek and the Communist resistance under Mao Zedong shared a fragile truce against the Japanese invasion. They cooperated when they had to. They turned their guns on each other when they could. The base game collapses both into one Chinese player. This expansion restores the original split.

Three Modes of Play

If you are using DLC 3, China must be played in one of three modes, agreed by the table at setup:

壹
Unified China
One player commands all of China. Both leader cards (Mao and Chiang) are available — the player chooses one. This is the base-game default.
貳
United Front
Two players share China cooperatively. Treasury and unit pool are shared. Each plays one of the two Chinese leaders. Disagreements on major actions are resolved by die roll.
參
Civil Truce
The dossier splits between Nationalist (KMT) and Communist (CCP). The two Chinese players act as independent powers. They may cooperate, betray, or stay in armed truce while jointly facing Japan.

Civil Truce — The 60 / 40 Split

When the Civil Truce mode is chosen, China's base dossier is divided between KMT and CCP as follows:

Civil Truce Rules

  • Both players are Chinese. They may declare team victory together at the China total (subject to the chosen game mode), but they may also turn on each other.
  • Capturing the other China. If a Chinese player captures a city or factory from the other Chinese player, the captured territory now produces for the captor. The KMT-vs-CCP civil war is real.
  • Versus Japan. Both Chinese players are independently at war with Japan from turn 1 unless they negotiate a separate peace.
  • Diplomacy. Either Chinese player may form an alliance with another power at any time. The other Chinese player is not bound by that alliance.

Occupied Nations

Korea, British India, and the Pacific dominions begin under another power. Here is how they break free.

The base game treats every nation as if it had political agency at the start of play. The historical reality was different. Korea was a Japanese colony. India and the Pacific dominions answered to the British Crown. This expansion adds the rules that let these nations act for themselves — and tells you what they must do to win their independence.

Korea — Under Japanese Occupation

Korea begins fully occupied by Japan. A player who chooses Korea does not act as a free nation at the start. Until Korea is liberated:
  • Korean territory, cities, and the Korean factory produce for Japan, not for the Korean player.
  • The Korean player may not train or move units freely; they may only build resistance using Kim Gu's Resistance Network ability (see Stats § D).
  • To play Korea independently, the Korean player (or an ally) must drive Japanese units out of Korean home territory and hold it for one full round.
  • Once liberated, Korea becomes a free nation and plays normally for the rest of the game.

British India & the Dominions — Under the Crown

British India and the Pacific dominions (Australia and New Zealand within Oceania) begin subordinate to the United Kingdom. Until they declare and survive independence:
  • The subordinate nation must follow the orders of the UK player on major actions — which wars to join, which territories to defend.
  • The subordinate nation keeps its own income and units, but cannot declare war on, or refuse to defend against, a target chosen by the UK player.
  • To play independently, the player must declare independence and survive one full round without losing their capital to British forces.
  • Once independence is declared and held, the nation acts entirely for itself — and may turn against its former master.
Best for advanced or cooperative play. Because they begin subordinate or occupied, Korea, British India, and an independent Oceania are best suited to experienced players, or to cooperative games where a Great Power ally helps engineer their liberation. A new player is usually better served by one of the eight Great Powers.
