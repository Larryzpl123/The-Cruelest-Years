Theater of Operations

Overview of the conflict, the duties of command, and how a war is won.

The world stands at the edge of its second great catastrophe. Empires falter, ideologies sharpen, and the appetite for land, gold, and glory has not yet been answered. In The Cruelest Years, each player takes one of eighteen nations to the field of the Second World War. You command its leaders, raise its armies, expand its industry, and steer its fate across a board of forty-one territories. Victory is measured in the soil you hold when the guns fall silent.

This edition is the streamlined ruleset. It is fast to teach, fast to play, and asymmetric by design — every nation has its own leader cards, its own unit trait, and its own strategic shape.

A game covering Europe + Asia requires 18 territories for solo victory (12 + 6) or 23 for team victory (15 + 8). This lets the table assemble a campaign of exactly the scale they want to play.
The Central Tension — Imperialism, colonialism, and nationalism have inflamed a generation of grievances over land, capital, and prestige. Every nation in the game holds at least one of these grievances; many hold all three. The game asks not who is right, but who can hold the ground when the smoke clears.

The Calendar of War

One turn is one month; ten years lie between the first shot and the last.

Each turn represents one calendar month. The game opens at the historical spark of the global conflict and may run as long as the table desires; the full historical span is October 1935 to September 1945.

Turn Order — The First Move

Because Italy struck the first blow of the war (October 1935, Ethiopia), Italy takes the first turn of the game. The Italian player then flips a coin to determine the direction of play:

  • Heads — play proceeds clockwise around the table.
  • Tails — play proceeds counter-clockwise.
Reversal Rule — At the start of every new round, the direction of play reverses. The last player of one round becomes the first player of the next, and so on. This prevents the same player from always reacting to the same neighbour, and prevents end-of-round kingmaking.

Order of a Turn — Four Phases

Each player resolves the following phases, in order, on their own turn:

  1. Income and Products Phase. Collect gold, population, and weapons from every city and factory under your control. Cities produce +5 gold and +1 population per turn. Factories produce +1 weapon per turn.
  2. Leader Phase. Use any leader or territorial ability. Passive abilities are always working and need not be declared; active abilities must be triggered now and may have a cost (gold, weapons, population) or a cooldown (CD) preventing reuse for a stated number of turns.
  3. Training Phase. Decide which military units to recruit and in which controlled territories to place them. A unit costs the weapons and population shown on the unit table. Newly trained units cannot move or attack on the turn they are created; they enter service next turn.
  4. Action Phase. Move and attack with previously trained units. A unit may attack after moving, but may not move after attacking unless its ability says otherwise.
House Rule (Short Campaign) — For a single sitting, begin in September 1939 (Invasion of Poland) and end whenever the table agrees, or by the first solo-victory threshold reached. For a quick demo game, play any scenario for 12 turns and award the win to whoever holds the most territory at the end of the year.

Special Rule · The Two Chinas

China occupies a unique political position in this period — a fragile coalition between the Nationalist government (KMT) under Chiang Kai-shek and the Communist resistance under Mao Zedong, both fighting Japan and each other. To reflect this, China may be played in any of three modes, agreed upon by the table before the campaign begins.

壹
Unified China
A single player commands all of China's resources, units, and decisions. Both leader cards (Mao and Chiang) are available to that one player.
貳
United Front
Two players share China. Both have full decision rights; the treasury and unit pool are shared. Each plays one of the two Chinese leaders. Disagreements on major actions are resolved by die roll.
參
Civil Truce
The dossier splits 60 / 40 between Nationalist (KMT, Chiang) and Communist (CCP, Mao). They may cooperate, betray, or remain in armed truce while jointly facing the Japanese invasion.

Cities, Factories & Resources

The three resources of war — gold, population, weapons — and the structures that produce them.

The streamlined economy of The Cruelest Years rests on three resources and two kinds of building. The math is simple by design — the strategy lies in where you place factories and how many turns you can hold a city against the enemy.

The Three Resources

🟡
Gold (Treasury)
The currency of the nation. Pays for ability costs, unit maintenance, and any agenda card with a money cost.
👥
Population
Manpower. Each new military unit consumes one or more population at training. Population is also the long-term measure of national strength.
🔫
Weapons
The output of factories. Combined with population, weapons form military units according to the unit table (§ VI).

Cities are the heart of a nation's civil economy. Each city under your control produces:

Cities are placed on the map at the start of the game and cannot be moved or rebuilt; if a city is captured by an enemy, its income belongs to the captor on the following turn. One city in each nation is designated the Capital — losing the capital is not a game-ending event by itself, but most Historical Plan and event cards reference it.

Factories — The War Engine

Factories produce 1 weapon per turn, every turn, with no choices to make. Each nation begins with the factory count printed in its dossier (§ IV) and may build more across the campaign. Factories are tied to their territory — if you lose the territory, you lose the factory.

A territory may hold at most 3 factories. The second factory in a territory costs +5 treasury and +1 turn; the third costs +10 treasury and +2 turns. Concentrated industry is expensive to grow.
Factories cannot be relocated. They are destroyed by a sustained Bomber strike on their territory or captured with the territory itself.
Strategic Vulnerability — Concentrating factories in one territory is efficient but dangerous. A single sustained bombing campaign on Berlin, the Ruhr, the Donbas, or Detroit can cripple a nation's entire production cycle. Distribute or fortify.

Maintenance Fees

Every military unit on the board costs treasury each turn to keep in the field, paid during the Income and Products Phase. Per-unit fees are listed in the unit table (§ VI). A unit whose maintenance cannot be paid is removed from the board.

Why the Old Inflation Track Is Gone — In earlier editions, printing treasury raised an inflation multiplier on every maintenance fee. The streamlined edition removes the multiplier and instead enforces discipline directly: if you cannot pay your maintenance, the unit goes home. Simple, sharp, no bookkeeping.

Order of Battle

The nine military units of the streamlined edition.

The roster has been consolidated. Where earlier editions distinguished Light from Heavy variants of tanks, boats, and planes, the streamlined edition presents one canonical unit per role. National flavour — heavier tanks, faster fighters, deadlier bombers — is delivered through the Unique Unit Trait (§ VIII) that modifies your nation's base units.

Reading the Table

Each unit lists its build cost (Weapon, Population) and its battlefield statistics (HP, Range, Attack, Speed, Fee). All units take one turn to train except the Aircraft Carrier (which takes longer to assemble in practice — handled by its higher cost rather than a separate timer in this edition).

Rules of Engagement

Training, movement, combat, and the targeting of the skies.

Training Units

Trained units are formed during the Training Phase. Pay the listed Weapons and Population from your stockpile; place an untrained figure on a controlled territory. The unit completes training at the start of your next turn and may then move and attack.

No Stacking — Each tile (territory) may contain only one military unit at a time. The earlier "stacks of three" rule is removed. Concentrate your striking power by careful sequencing of attacks, not by piling up tokens.

Movement & Speed

A unit's Speed is the number of tiles it may traverse in a single Action Phase. A unit may attack after moving, but may not move after attacking (unless a leader ability or agenda card specifically permits).

Combat & HP Coins

Each unit's HP is tracked by HP coins placed beneath the figure. When a unit is attacked, remove HP coins equal to the attacker's Attack value. A unit with no remaining HP coins is destroyed and removed from the board.

Range & The Over-Range Strike

Range is the maximum number of tiles between attacker and target for a normal strike. A commander may also attempt an over-range strike: declaring an attack against a target one tile beyond the unit's printed range. To succeed, the attacker flips a coin.

  • Heads — the over-range strike hits normally.
  • Tails — the strike misses; the attack is wasted but the unit's action is still spent.

Over-range strikes may only extend a unit's range by one tile. They cannot chain.

Air Warfare

Aircraft operate in a layer of their own. Only Planes and Anti-Air Artillery may target aircraft. All other units must endure aerial bombardment without reply. Bombers in particular can shatter industrial centres — sustained Bomber raids can destroy factories on a captured-or-defended territory.

Aircraft Carriers — The carrier's Range 1, Attack 1 stats are for self-defence only. Its real value is two-fold: it launches friendly Planes from the deep ocean, and it carries up to six land units across water. Without carriers, a Pacific war is impossible to win.

Leaders & Unique Units

The face, the doctrine, and the trademark formation of each nation. The heart of the asymmetric game.

Every nation in The Cruelest Years carries three signatures of its own. Leader cards grant one or two named individuals with passive or active abilities. The Unique Unit Trait modifies the nation's base units according to its historical industrial specialty. Together these define how the nation feels on the board, and they are the chief source of the game's replayability.

How Leaders Work — A nation has one to three leader cards drawn from its historical leadership. At the start of the campaign, the player chooses one leader to govern with for the entire game. The choice is binding — there is no swap mid-game except via specific event cards. Leader abilities are tagged PASSIVE (always on), ACTIVE (declared on a turn, may have cost and cooldown), or HYBRID (a permanent effect plus an activatable component).

Agendas, Policies & Historical Plans

A small deck of strategic doctrines layered on top of leader cards. Still in design.

Beyond their leader cards and unit traits, each nation will hold three Agenda cards representing the deliberate strategic doctrines of its wartime leadership. These are not required for play — the streamlined edition is complete without them — but they add a second layer of strategy and are the home for the more flavourful national mechanics (food policy, mobilization edicts, alliance frameworks, etc.).

How a Card Works

  1. Activation. Declare during the Leader Phase. Pay any cost in gold, weapons, population, or units.
  2. Effect. Resolve immediately, or persist for the stated number of turns.
  3. Cooldown. Once played, set the card aside for its stated cooldown.
  4. Conditional Plans. Some cards may only be played if a precondition is met (e.g. "must be at war with a Great Power").

Worked Example · Germany's Blitzkrieg

Germany's Tiger Tank trait already grants +2 Speed as an active ability with cooldown 6 (§ VIII). The Agenda card layer extends this idea to other doctrines.

Card Library — In Development. The full deck of 54 cards (three per nation × eighteen nations) is still being designed and balanced. Each card will follow the cost / effect / cooldown / condition format above. Provisional drafts under consideration include:
  • Soviet · Scorched Earth. Burn the improvements of a region to deny them to an invader. Cost: 5 gold. Cooldown: 8 turns.
  • USA · Lend-Lease. Gift weapons or treasury to an ally. Cost: 15 gold to give 10 weapons or 10 treasury. Cooldown: 4 turns.
  • Japan · Bushidō Charge. All Infantry +2 Attack for one turn; –1 HP next turn. Cost: free. Cooldown: 6 turns.
  • UK · Convoy Defense. Reveal one enemy naval region per turn for 3 turns. Cost: 5 gold. Cooldown: 5 turns.
  • China · Long March. Move any number of friendly units 2 tiles toward the interior without combat penalty. Cost: free. Cooldown: 10 turns.

V. The Open Bout (Quickplay)
Belligerents: Any 2 powers, agreed by the table.
Turns: 12 (one in-game year).
Players: 2.
Win: Whoever holds the most territory at the end of the year. Tied? Whoever has the higher (Population + Treasury) total.
Premise: Tutorial-length introduction. Use for first plays.
Designer's Note — Theatre modules are not miniature versions of the full game; they are real campaigns at smaller scale. Each ships with its own subset of the events deck (Pearl Harbor is pre-resolved in the Pacific module, etc.) and its own balance adjustments. The full 120-turn flagship is the only way to feel the strategic linkage between fronts — Stalingrad relieving Britain, Pearl Harbor dragging America into Europe.

Designer's Field Notes

A candid record of the questions every wargame about this war must answer.

No game set in the years 1935–1945 can be made innocently. The designer of The Cruelest Years wrote down the questions below before placing a single counter on the board, and revisits them with every edition of the rulebook. They are reproduced here so that every commander who plays the game knows what was being attempted, and what was being refused.

What historical event are you considering?
The Second World War, from the Italian invasion of Ethiopia in October 1935 to the Japanese surrender in September 1945. 120 turns of one month each.
What is the central conflict or tension?
Growing imperialism, colonialism, and nationalism produced overlapping conflicts of land, money, and prestige between countries throughout the world. In the streamlined edition the central tension is operationalized as a contest for territory — the soil your nation can hold at the end of the war.
Who are the major players?
Eighteen nations, weighted to the European and Asian theatres where the war was fought hardest. Nine European powers; two Asian; seven distributed across the Americas, Africa, the Pacific, the Middle East, and South Asia. Each nation carries one or more named historical leaders.
What was at stake?
At the level of leaders, the stake was land, money, and power. At the level of populations, the stake was survival. The streamlined game expresses this as the simple, brutal question: who holds the ground when the smoke clears?
Is there one moment where a different decision could have changed everything?
There are several. The game's optional events deck (§ X) treats three as formal decision points: the German – Soviet Non-Aggression Pact, the German invasion of the Soviet Union, and the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor.
Why does the game work?
Because the asymmetry is real. Sixteen leader pools, sixteen unit traits, four theatres, and a coin-flip turn-direction at game start mean every campaign tells a different story. The streamlined rules keep the moving parts few; the variety lives in the cards, not the bookkeeping.
What could go wrong?
Two persistent design problems. First, the brutality of the Axis regimes — atrocities, occupation, genocide — cannot be performed as moves and is not present mechanically. The streamlined game represents the cost only through territory loss and historical events. Second, balance: some leader cards are visibly stronger than others (FDR, Reza Shah, Hindenburg) and will require playtest revision. The current solution is the team-victory mechanic, which lets weaker neighbours combine against a strong central power.
Is it ethical for this to be a board game?
The event is ethical to use as a game so long as the approach is comprehensive and educational — to learn from history's mistakes, not to re-enact them as spectacle. The rulebook, the events deck, and these designer's notes are written with that intention.
