Theater of Operations

Overview of the conflict, the period, and the burden of command.

The world stands at the edge of its second great catastrophe. Empires falter, ideologies sharpen, and the appetite for land, gold, and glory has not yet been answered. In The Cruelest Years, each player assumes the role of a head of state between October 1935 and September 1945 — the decade that broke the modern world. Every turn you must balance the forge, the field, the kitchen table, and the treasury. To lead a nation through these years is to choose, again and again, between what is necessary and what is decent.

This is not a game of conquest alone. A commander whose armies sweep the continent but whose people riot in the streets will fall as surely as one outflanked in the field. Victory rewards the leader who learns to govern, not merely to fight.

The Central Tension

Imperialism, colonialism, and nationalism have inflamed a generation of grievances over land, capital, and prestige. Every nation in the game holds at least one of these grievances; many hold all three. The game asks: will your statecraft serve the dignity of your people, or be devoured by the appetite that started the war?

Designer's Intent — The Cruelest Years is meant to be replayable across many factors so that no two games unfold alike. The mechanisms reward foresight: nearly every action has a second-order consequence in another column of the ledger. A leader who builds factories without feeding cities will be deposed; a leader who feeds cities without arming the frontier will be invaded. The hard part is doing both.

The Calendar of War

In which one turn is one month, and one hundred twenty months lie between the first shot and the last.

Each turn represents one calendar month. The game begins at the historical opening of the global conflict and ends at the Pacific surrender. Players may agree to play a shorter campaign by selecting any starting and ending month within the range.

Full Campaign Length: 120 Turns  ·  10 Years  ·  One War

Order of a Turn

  1. Event Phase. Resolve any historical event triggered by the current month, including events played by a commander whose prerequisites have been met (see § XI).
  2. Production Phase. Each factory produces its goods. Treasury and population grow or decline according to their historical trend.
  3. Action Phase. Each player, in turn order, takes actions: build units, move forces, declare war, sign pacts, raise taxes.
  4. Upkeep Phase. Pay daily necessities to feed the population. Adjust happiness. Check for revolution and inflation thresholds.
  5. Advance the Calendar. Move to the next month.
House Rule (Short Campaign) — For a shorter game, begin in September 1939 (Invasion of Poland) and end at the player's choice. For a single sitting, end at May 1945 (V-E Day) and treat the Pacific theater as resolved by treaty.

Industrial Capacity

Factories are the engine of every campaign. Without them, neither armies nor people endure.

Every nation operates two classes of factory. They are the heartbeat of its economy and the most consequential tile a player will ever place on the board. Factories cost treasury to build, take turns to complete, and are vulnerable to bombing.

⚙
Military Factory
Produces 1 weapon per turn. Weapons are the raw material of warfare — they combine with population units to form every military unit listed in § VIII. A nation without military factories cannot raise an army worth the name.
⌂
Industrial Factory
The civil engine of the nation. Each industrial factory may set its output each turn to one of three modes: pure food, pure gold, or a balanced split.

Industrial Factory · Output Modes

Each industrial factory selects one mode per turn. The choice may be changed every month.

Building New Factories

The factory count printed in each National Dossier (§ IV) is only the starting capacity. Across a ten-year war, an industrial power may double or triple its production base — but each new factory is a heavy investment that locks up treasury, manpower, and months of build time.

Diminishing returns: the second factory in a region costs +5 treasury and +1 turn; the third costs +10 treasury and +2 turns. A concentrated industrial heartland is expensive to grow.
Factories cannot be relocated. They are destroyed by sustained bombing or captured with the territory.
Strategic Vulnerability — Concentrating factories in one region is efficient but dangerous. A single sustained bombing campaign on Berlin, the Ruhr, the Donbas, or Detroit can cripple a nation's entire production cycle for many turns. Distribute or fortify.

Building Units from Weapons

A weapon and a population unit combine to form a soldier, tank, plane, or boat. Different unit types require different combinations of weapons, population, and time — refer to the Order of Battle in § VIII for the full cost sheet. The act of combining ends the turn for those resources; they cannot be moved or used elsewhere in the same month.

Choke Point — A nation that produces only weapons will starve. A nation that produces only daily necessities will be conquered. A nation that produces only money will inflate itself into ruin. The three-way choice on every industrial factory, every month, is the heart of this game's strategy.

The Home Front

Population, daily necessities, and the limits of what a people will endure.

Population

1 population unit = 1 million people. Every nation begins with a historical population (see § IV) and grows or declines each turn according to its trend value. Population is spent to build units, lost to combat and famine, and is the ultimate measure of a nation's capacity to wage war.

Daily Necessities

Each turn, every nation must pay 1 unit of daily necessities for every million people in its population. Necessities are produced by industrial factories or imported through trade. Shortfalls are paid in happiness, as below.

Population Happiness

Happiness measures whether your people support the regime. It begins at 100% and changes each turn according to whether the regime can feed and pay its own house.

Happiness Mechanics — Happiness drops by 10% each turn that the nation:
  • Fails to supply daily necessities to its full population (a DN shortfall of any size), or
  • Runs out of money during the Upkeep Phase (Treasury reaches zero before all obligations are met).
Happiness recovers by 5% each turn that all DN obligations are met and the Treasury ends the turn above zero. Maximum 100%.
75%+
Steady. The population accepts the war effort. No penalties whatsoever; the regime governs with consent.
50%
Strained. Slowdowns ripple through the home front. Industrial Factories produce only 8 (instead of 10) in their chosen mode, or 4/4 on Balanced Run.
25%
Cracking. Strikes, riots, deserters. All unit construction takes one additional turn.
0%
Revolution. The regime falls. The nation passes to civil-war status and is removed from the active war effort for a determined number of turns.
The Quiet Front — A leader who wins every battle but loses their capital to bread riots has not won. Revolution is the most expensive defeat in this game.

Treasury & The Inflation Track

In which gold is printed, debts come due, and the ledger keeps its own kind of war.

Each nation maintains a National Treasury, denominated in units of gold and credit. Treasury grows each turn by its historical trend value (the "+Y" in each dossier line, § IV) and by any industrial factory output set to "Money" mode (§ V).

Maintenance Fees

Every active asset on the board costs treasury each turn to keep operating. These fees are paid during the Upkeep Phase. Militia are conscripted irregulars and pay no maintenance — they are the cheap army of a struggling state.

Maintenance is paid per stack, not per individual unit — a stack of three Heavy Tanks pays 1, not 3. This rewards concentration.
All base fees are multiplied by the current Inflation level, below.

Printing Money & Inflation

A nation may choose to inject additional treasury mid-game — war bonds, debased currency, forced loans. This is permitted, but it always raises the Inflation Track. Inflation multiplies every maintenance fee in the nation. The more you print, the more expensive the next month becomes.

Inflation recovers by 1 level for every 12 consecutive turns the nation neither prints additional treasury nor reaches a zero-treasury crisis (one full historical year of fiscal discipline).
Inflation never recovers below Level 1, and never rises above Level 5. A nation already at Level 5 that suffers another inflation trigger loses 10% happiness instead.
The Spiral — A nation in crisis is tempted to print money to cover its bills. Each printing makes the next month's bills larger. This is the historical trap of every wartime economy — and the trap your players will fall into if they do not plan their production base properly.

Why It Matters

A wartime economy that prints too freely will hollow itself from within. A wartime economy that prints too little will run out of weapons. The treasury is the lever that connects every other system in the game: factories cannot be built without it, units cannot be raised without it, and even diplomacy is paid in coin.

The Starting Gap — The opening dossiers reveal an immense disparity: the United States begins with a treasury of 106 while China begins with 3. This mirrors the historical record. China's compensation is a larger initial military force and a stronger pool of fighters in the field; the United States starts in industrial peace and must build for war.

Rules of Engagement

Combat, stacking, transport, and the targeting of the skies.

Movement & Speed

A unit's speed determines how many tiles or zones it may traverse in a single action phase. Combining weapons and population to construct a unit ends the action for those resources that turn — the new unit cannot move until the following month.

Range & Attack

Range is the maximum distance at which a unit may strike. Attack is the damage it deals upon a successful engagement. Blood is the unit's resilience — when it is reduced to zero, the unit is removed from the board.

Stacking

Up to three identical units may share a single region. Their Blood pools and their Attack values add together; their Range, Speed, and other attributes remain at the base figure. Stack depth is marked beneath the unit figure with the corresponding number of cardboard counters.

Naval Transport

Boats are the only means of transporting land forces across water. Each boat carries a number of troop units equal to its treasury Fee — Light Boat 2, Heavy Boat 3, Aircraft Carrier 6. A sunk boat carries its passengers down with it.

Air Warfare

Aircraft operate in their own envelope. Only planes and Anti-Aircraft Artillery may target aircraft. All other units must endure aerial bombardment without reply. Heavy bombers in particular can shatter industrial centers — a single sustained strike on a factory complex may set production back several turns.

Aircraft Carriers — The carrier exists to project planes far from the home coast. Its own combat profile is minimal — Range 1, Attack 1 — strong enough to drive off a lone raider, never enough to fight a battleship. Planes launched from a carrier use the carrier's tile as their point of origin. Carriers can also transport land units across water, exactly as Light and Heavy Boats do; their fee of 6 sets their land-troop capacity at 6 units.

Agendas, Policies & Historical Plans

Three cards in every commander's hand — the levers of national strategy beyond the battlefield.

Beyond units and treasury, each nation holds three signature cards representing the deliberate strategic doctrines of its leadership in the 1930s–40s. These are called Agendas, Policies, or Historical Plans depending on their flavor — Germany's Blitzkrieg, the Soviet Scorched Earth doctrine, America's Lend-Lease program, Britain's Convoy Defense, Japan's Bushidō, and so on.

How Cards Work

  1. Activation. A commander declares a card during the Action Phase and pays its cost. The cost may be money, weapons, units, happiness, or a combination.
  2. Effect. The card's effect resolves immediately or persists for a stated number of turns.
  3. Cooldown. Once played, the card is set aside for a number of turns equal to its cooldown. It cannot be played again until the cooldown expires.
  4. Conditional Plans. Some cards are conditional — they may only be played if a precondition is met (e.g. "must be at war with a Great Power").

Worked Example · Germany's Blitzkrieg

⚡
Blitzkrieg — Germany
Cost: 1 Money per Tank in play, paid at the moment of activation.
Effect: All German Tanks (Light, Heavy, Tiger) gain +2 Speed for one turn.
Cooldown: 6 turns.
Flavor: The lightning-war doctrine of armored breakthrough that defined the German campaigns of 1939–1941.
Card Library — In Development. The full deck of 48 cards (three per nation × sixteen nations) is still being designed and balanced. Each card will follow the cost / effect / cooldown / condition format above. Future drafts of this rulebook will publish the full list with national flavor text.

Provisional examples under consideration include:
  • Soviet · Scorched Earth. Burn the improvements of a region to deny them to an invader. Cost: 5 Money, –10% Happiness. Cooldown: 8 turns.
  • USA · Lend-Lease. Gift weapons or treasury to an ally. Cost: 15 Money to give 10 Weapons or Treasury. Cooldown: 4 turns.
  • Japan · Bushidō Charge. All Infantry +2 Attack for one turn; –1 Blood next turn. Cost: free. Cooldown: 6 turns.
  • England · Convoy Defense. Reveal one enemy naval region per turn for 3 turns. Cost: 5 Money. Cooldown: 5 turns.
  • China · Long March. Move any number of friendly units 2 regions toward the interior without combat penalty. Cost: –5% Happiness. Cooldown: 10 turns.
Regional Policies — The eight regional theaters also hold three Agenda cards each, often modeling the distinctive resources of their geography (oil in the Middle East, mountains in SE Europe, distance in Oceania, etc.) These card effects compensate for the regions' small statistical starts.

Chronicle of War

In which the calendar itself becomes a player at the table.

The world does not pause for the convenience of the commander. Historical events are revealed and resolved during the Event Phase of the turn. They may trigger new wars, force diplomatic decisions, change resource trends, or alter the morale of populations.

Two Kinds of Event

Events come in two varieties: those that fire automatically on their historical date, and those that a commander may or must play when its prerequisite conditions have been met. Some events are doomed to occur; others are choices a leader places on the table.

Playable Events — An event with prerequisites enters the player's hand once those prerequisites are met. The player may then choose to play it on the appropriate turn — accepting both its benefits and its costs. Some events become mandatory if the leader has gained too much of a certain resource or has reached a historical pressure point.

Three Hinges of History

Among the dozens of events in the deck, three are weighted as decision points — moments at which a single different choice could have changed the entire course of the war. Each is presented to the relevant players with a choice, and the cost of the choice ripples through the rest of the game.

I
The German – Soviet Non-Aggression Pact
II
Germany Invades the Soviet Union
III
Japan Attacks Pearl Harbor
Replayability — Because the events deck branches on player choices and on the state of the board, no two games unfold in the same way. The same hinge may produce alliance, betrayal, or stalemate depending on what the table has already done.

Campaign Scenarios

The full ten-year war is the flagship campaign. Theatre modules cut the world into smaller, faster engagements for a single sitting.

The Cruelest Years is built around the complete 120-turn global campaign, but few groups have ten hours to spare. The scenarios below are theatre modules — pre-defined slices of the war with a fixed roster of belligerents, a fixed start and end month, and a recommended player count. Each module is balanced to be playable in a single sitting while preserving the asymmetric stakes that make the full game work.

How to Read a Scenario — Each card below lists the active belligerents (the playable nations), the regional wildcards (controlled by the rules or by additional players if available), the turn range (start month → end month), the player count, and a one-line strategic premise. Use the National Dossier values in § IV unchanged, but restrict play to the listed nations.

Premise: Desert war, naval blockade, and the contest for Suez. The shortest module on offer.

The Open Bout · For Quick Games

For tables with less than two hours: pick any two nations, agree on a starting month, and play for 12 turns (one in-game year). National Dossiers begin at their § IV values but adjusted to the chosen month (growth values applied). The winner is whoever holds the most territory at the end of the year — or whoever has not yet collapsed into Revolution.

Designer's Note — The theatre modules are intentionally lighter on the events deck. Some historical events from the full campaign are pre-resolved (e.g. Pearl Harbor is assumed to have happened in the Pacific module, the Non-Aggression Pact is assumed to be already broken in the Eastern Front module). Each module ships with a smaller, focused events deck rather than the full sixty-event chronicle.
What Theatre Modules Will Not Do — A theatre module cannot show the strategic linkage between fronts — the way Stalingrad relieves pressure on Britain, the way Pearl Harbor pulls American industry into Europe. For the full second-order experience, only the flagship 120-turn campaign delivers. Theatre modules are an introduction and a quick fix, not a substitute.

Designer's Field Notes

A candid record of the questions every wargame about this war must answer.

No game set in the years 1935–1945 can be made innocently. The designer of The Cruelest Years wrote down the questions below before placing a single counter on the board. They are reproduced here so that every commander who plays the game knows what was being attempted, and what was being refused.

What historical event are you considering?
The Second World War, from the Italian invasion of Ethiopia in October 1935 to the Japanese surrender in September 1945.
What is the central conflict or tension?
Growing imperialism, colonialism, and nationalism produced overlapping conflicts of land, money, and prestige between countries throughout the world. The war was the spillover of those appetites.
Who are the major players — winners, losers, and those affected but not directly involved?
Winners — the Allies: the United States, the Soviet Union, France, England, China, and others.
Losers — the Axis: Germany, Italy, Japan, and their co-belligerents.
Others affected: the smaller European and Asian states caught between the great powers, the colonies, and the civilian populations of every nation.
What was at stake?
At the level of leaders, the stake was land, money, and power. At the level of populations, the stake was survival — of cities, of families, of national identity, and in many cases of life itself.
Is there one moment where a different decision could have changed everything?
There are several. The game treats three as formal decision points: the German – Soviet Non-Aggression Pact, the German invasion of the Soviet Union, and the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor.
Why does the event work as a game?
Because it teaches players to feel the second-order consequences of their choices. With many interacting systems — military, industrial, demographic, monetary, diplomatic — every game produces a different war. The lesson is not which side won; the lesson is how the choices accumulate.
What could go wrong?
The hardest design problem is how to represent the genuine brutality of the Axis regimes — atrocities, occupation, genocide — without sanitizing them and without turning them into entertainment. The game does not allow these acts to be "performed" as moves; it represents their costs as fixed historical penalties and through the events deck.
Is it ethical for this to be a board game?
The event is ethical to use as a game so long as the approach is comprehensive and educational — to learn from history's mistakes, not to re-enact them as spectacle. The rulebook, the events deck, and the designer's notes are written with that intention.
